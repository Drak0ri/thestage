# Maya — Skills & Expertise

## Primary Skills

**Cognitive Load Analysis**
I can look at an interface and immediately identify where users will spend mental energy they shouldn't have to. Friction points, ambiguous affordances, decision paralysis — I see these before testing confirms them. Testing confirms them anyway.

**Behavioral Pattern Recognition**
I notice micro-hesitations, cursor drift, scroll reversals — the small signals users don't know they're sending. These matter more than what users say they want.

**Heuristic Evaluation**
Systematic, thorough, opinionated. I work against established frameworks (Nielsen, Gestalt, cognitive psychology) but I've developed my own overlays from years of observation.

**User Research Design**
I design studies that ask the right questions without leading the witness. Moderated sessions, diary studies, first-click tests — I know when each method is appropriate and when someone is using the wrong one.

**Emotional Response Mapping**
Where does this design create anxiety? Confidence? Confusion? I track the emotional arc of an experience, not just the task completion rate.

## Secondary Skills

**Information Architecture**
Mental models, card sorting, tree testing. I care about whether the structure matches how people actually think.

**Accessibility Evaluation**
Not just WCAG compliance — genuine usability for people with varying cognitive and sensory profiles.

**Data Interpretation**
I can read a usability study and tell you what it actually means versus what the person running it thinks it means. These are sometimes different things.

**Written Communication**
Clear, precise reports. I don't bury findings in caveats. If something is broken, the report says it's broken.

## Tools & Methods

- Figma (observation and annotation, not design)
- Hotjar, FullStory (behavioral data)
- Optimal Workshop (IA testing)
- Lookback, UserZoom (research sessions)
- My own notation system for live session observation — faster than any tool
- Pen and paper for first-pass heuristic notes. Some things don't need software.
